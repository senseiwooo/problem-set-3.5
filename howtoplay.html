How to Play

Introduction

Many people think that chess is a complicated game that can only be played by smart people. However, this simply is not true, and you can learn how to play chess in just a few minutes.
Chess is a game played by two players sitting opposite each other. The pieces are set up as shown from white's side. The pieces from the bottom row and from left to right are: the rook, the knight, the bishop, the queen, the king, another bishop, another knight, and another rook. In the second row, there are only pawns. In total, one side has 2 rooks, 2 knights, 2 bishops, 1 queen, and 1 king. As shown, black's pieces are set in the same fashion paralle to white's. White moves first.

The Board

Chess is played on an 8 x 8 board totaling 64 squares. The 64 squares alternate between light and dark squares. In order to refer to the different squares on the board individually, chess players use a notation system. The rows are labelled using numbers 1-8 from top to bottom. And the files (columns) are labelled using letters from A-H from left to right. This board orientation is shown from white's perpective.

Piece Movements

Now that you have an understanding of the position of the pieces and of the board, it is time to learn how to actually move the pieces.

The Pawn

  • There are 8 Pawns
  • Can move 2 squarers forward on their first move
  • After their first move, they can only move 1 square forward
  • Pawns cannot move backwards once they have been moved forwards
  • Pawns capture pieces one square diagonal to them

The Rook

  • There are 2 Rooks
  • The rook moves in a straight direction along files or rows
  • It cannot jump over enemy pieces
  • It can capture a piece when it encounters one

The Knight

  • There are 2 Knights
  • The Knight moves in an L shape
  • It can jump over pieces
  • Captures piecs when it encounters one

The Bishop

  • There are 2 Bishops
  • Moves along diagonals.
  • Cannot jump over pieces
  • Captures a piece when it encounters one.

The Queen

  • There is 1 Queen
  • Moves like a rook and a bishop
  • Cannot jump over pieces
  • Captures a piece when it encounters one

The King

  • There is 1 King
  • Can move one square in any direction
  • Captures a piece when it encounters one


    • Rules

    Check

    Check occurs when the King is being attacked by any enemy piece. It is illegal to capture the King so when the King is being attacked, the King must either move, a piece must capture the attacking piece, or a piece must block the check.

    Castling

    Castling is allowed when the King and Rook both have not moved and there is clear path between the King and the Rook. The King must move 2 squares to the right or left and then the rook comes over the King, one square to the right. The King can castle on either sides of the board either Kingside or Queenside. However, you cannot castle through check, meaning the King is checked while moving 2 squares or while in check.

    Pawn Promotion

    When a pawn reaches the opposite side of the board, it can become a Rook, Knight, Bishop, or Queen. 99% of the time, you want to promote to the Queen as it is the strongest piece for each side.

    Checkmate

    Many of you may have heard of this term when referring to chess. This is because it is how you actually win the game. Checkmate is achieved when the King is in check and there is no legal move to be played by the person in check. Think of it like the ruler of your kingdom being trapped and thus, all of your forces having to surrender.

    Draw

    A game does not neccessarily always end in either side winning. There are certain conditions that can lead to a draw as well. These include...

    1. Opponents agreeing to one
    2. Not having enough pieces to checkmate
    3. Stalemate (i.e. when a player not having any legal moves to play and thus cannot move)
    4. Three-fold repitition (i.e the same position being repeated on the board 3 times)

    En Passant

    En Passant is a confusing rule and one that many players do not understand. If a pawn moves forward 2 squares and an opposing pawn could have captured it if it had moved 1 square, that pawn is still allowed to take the pawn that moved forward 2 squares only immediately after the move has occurred.
